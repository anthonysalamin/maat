# MAAT Investor Pitch — Review & Improvement Plan

## Verdict in one line

The raw material is strong (real founder-market fit, genuine product depth, honest framing), but it's packaged as a 13-page reference document, not a pitch. An investor decides in ~3 minutes; this asks for 15. **Cut it to ~8–9 pages, say each thing once, and stop apologizing for the lack of traction nine separate times.**

---

## 1. The core problem: it reads like documentation, not a pitch

You diagnosed this correctly — it's long and dense. But the deeper issue is *why*: the document is organized like a wiki (16 numbered sections covering every angle) rather than like an argument (problem → why now → solution → proof → ask). A pitch is a persuasion sequence, not a table of contents.

**Target length: 8–9 content pages.** Each page should advance the argument, not re-describe the product from a new angle.

---

## 2. The repetition audit (this is the biggest fix)

The same ideas appear over and over. Here's where, so you can find and merge them:

**The HARMAR origin story** is told in full in sections **2, 3, 13, and 14** — and referenced in 1 and 4. An investor reads "born from a real construction project in Bahia, Brazil" five times. Tell it **once**, in the founder slide, and make it land hard.

**The fragmentation list** ("WhatsApp, email, Excel, paper") appears in sections **1, 2, and 5**. It belongs in the problem slide only.

**The disclaimer "illustrative planning figures, not audited results — see Disclaimers"** appears as an inline note after roughly **nine** sections, *plus* a full 1.5-page disclaimer section at the end. This is the single most damaging repetition — see point 4.

**The tagline "from revenue to margin" / "intent to settlement"** appears in sections 2, 4, 6, 7, 10, 13, 15. Pick the one or two places it carries weight.

**The Swiss differentiators** (multilingual FR/DE/IT, data residency, compliance, cheaper than Procore) are listed in sections **7, 8, 9, and 10**. Consolidate into the competitive slide.

**The product capabilities** are described in sections **4, 5, 6, 8, and 12** — five separate passes at "what it does."

---

## 3. Five sections are really one: collapse the product story

Sections 4 (The solution), 5 (Before & after), 6 (How it works), and 12 (Product in practice) all answer the same question: *what is it and what does it do?* You don't need four slides for that.

**Merge into two:**
- **One "Solution" slide** — the Intent → Execution → Outcome model (from section 6) plus the "one workspace / one audit trail" promise. This is your clearest, most concrete framing; lead with it.
- **One "Before / After" slide** — the table from section 5 is genuinely good and does the work of sections 1, 4, and 6 combined. Keep it tight.

Section 12 ("Product in practice") is confused: it links to live in-product pages that don't work in a PDF. Either embed 2–3 real screenshots (far more persuasive than feature links) or cut it and offer a live demo. Investors want to *see* the product, not read a list of module names.

---

## 4. The disclaimers are actively hurting you

Honesty about being pre-revenue is good and rare — keep the spirit. But repeating "illustrative, not audited, not researched, investors will test them" ~9 times inline, then again across a 1.5-page disclaimer section, does the opposite of what you intend. It reads as a lack of confidence and makes the reader distrust *every* number, including the credible ones.

**Fix:**
- Remove every inline "these are illustrative figures" note.
- Replace with **one small footer line** on the deck: *"Market, pricing and efficiency figures are planning assumptions, not audited results."*
- Keep a single short assumptions appendix (half a page max) for the diligence-minded — sources you'll validate (BFS, SBV, Eurostat), the CHF 120/hr basis, etc.

The "no commercial customers yet" fact is your actual story — frame it **once, confidently**, on the validation slide: *"Product is built and shipped; the next milestone is three signed Swiss design partners. That's what this round funds."* Said once, it's disarming honesty. Said nine times, it's a flashing warning light.

---

## 5. Bury-the-lead problem: your best assets are hidden

Two of your strongest cards are buried late and underplayed:

- **Engineering depth (section 14):** 777 migrations, 162 tables, 317 RPCs, 6 locales in parity, 112 doc pages. For a pre-revenue startup this is unusually real — it de-risks execution hard. This deserves prominence, not page 10.
- **Founder-market fit (section 13):** EPFL engineer, 20+ years, SAP/procurement/finance, *and* he personally ran the construction project that created the product. That's a top-tier founder story. It should come earlier and be told as a narrative, not a bullet list.

## 6. Proposed structure (16 sections → 9 slides)

| # | Slide | Pulls from | Notes |
|---|-------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **Problem** | 1 + Swiss reality | Fragmentation list lives here only. One sharp line: "A firm only learns a project lost money once it's over." |
| 2 | **Why now** | 9 | Three converging trends. Strong, keep nearly as-is. |
| 3 | **Solution** | 4 + 6 | Intent → Execution → Outcome. Lead with the model. |
| 4 | **Before / After** | 5 | The table. Cut everything redundant with slide 3. |
| 5 | **Why customers buy** | 7 + 8 | Money/time/risk + the competitive table. Merge the Swiss differentiators in here. |
| 6 | **Founder & origin** | 2 + 3 + 13 | Tell HARMAR *once*, as the founder's story. Your strongest emotional + credibility beat. |
| 7 | **Validation & product depth** | 14 | Lead with engineering depth. Be honest about traction *here, once.* |
| 8 | **Market, model & GTM** | 11 | TAM/SAM/SOM, pricing, the founder-reachable channels (genuinely credible — keep). |
| 9 | **Vision & ask** | 15 | The ladder, the CHF 500k, the 18-month milestones. Clear and last. |

That's 9 slides + appendix vs. the current 13 pages of 16 sections.

## 7. Smaller fixes worth making

- **Density:** many slides are wall-to-wall prose. Investor slides should be skimmable — one headline assertion + 3–5 supporting points + one visual. The headlines you already have (e.g. "A construction firm only learns a project lost money once it is over") are excellent; let them carry more weight with less body text around them.
- **TAM/SAM/SOM:** CHF 6.5B → 0.9B → 5.4M is a believable funnel, but show the one-line logic visually rather than burying it in a bullet list of assumptions.
- **The ask:** the CHF 500k → ~50 customers → ~CHF 0.7M ARR → de-risked Series A logic is good. Make it the closing punchline, very visible. Right now it competes with the disclaimer text on the same page.
- **Cut "MAAT advantage" (section 10) as a standalone** — the moat argument (connected data model, governance, multi-year logic) folds naturally into the competitive slide.
- **One tagline, chosen deliberately.** "Revenue-to-margin operating system for project-based companies" is good. Use it on the cover and the vision slide — not on seven slides.

---

## What's already working (don't lose it)

- The problem is concrete and the pain is real and specific.
- "Don't sell features — sell money, time, and risk" is exactly the right instinct for Swiss SME buyers.
- The founder-reachable acquisition channels (network, EPFL alumni, real supplier relationships) are far more credible than generic "market access."
- The honesty about being pre-traction — *once you stop repeating it* — builds trust.
- The Before/After table and the Intent→Execution→Outcome model are your clearest assets.

---

**Bottom line:** you're not missing content — you have too much, repeated too often, with confidence-sapping disclaimers stapled to every number. Halve the length, say each thing once, lead with founder + product depth, and put the ask where it can't be missed.
